Participant Information and Consent

Study title: How people evaluate statements and information online
Investigators: Yen-Chieh Liao, Linette Lim, Slava Jankin
Institution: School of Government, University of Birmingham
Ethics reference: ERN_5773-Mar2026

We are researchers at the University of Birmingham studying how people evaluate statements and claims they encounter online. This survey takes approximately 10–12 minutes.

What you will do: You will read three short statements and answer questions about each one. You will rate how accurate each statement seems and how likely you would be to share it. You will also answer some background questions.

Some statements may not be accurate. We will give you the correct information for all three statements at the end of the survey, before you receive your completion code.

Your participation is voluntary. You can stop at any time by closing your browser. If you wish to withdraw your submitted data, contact Yen-Chieh Liao (y.liao.1@bham.ac.uk) with your Prolific participant ID within 14 days of completing the survey.

Your data: Your survey responses will be stored securely on University of Birmingham infrastructure. Your Prolific ID will be permanently deleted within 14 days of data collection closing. Anonymised responses will also be used in a computational modelling component of this research, in which an AI agent based on your anonymised profile will be used to simulate responses. No personally identifiable information is used in this process. Full details are available in the Participant Information Sheet (ERN_5773-Mar2026), available from y.liao.1@bham.ac.uk.

Debrief

Thank You for Completing This Survey

This study was examining how people respond to factual information about statements they have seen. The three statements you evaluated were examples of claims that circulate online but are inaccurate. Here is the accurate information for each:

“The UK government spends about 5% of its annual budget on scientific research and development.”

UK government spending on scientific research and development:

The UK government spent approximately £17.4 billion on R&D in 2023, approximately 1.3% of total government expenditure — not 5%.

Source: ONS (2023).

“The UK government currently spends around 10% of its annual budget on overseas foreign aid.”

UK government spending on overseas foreign aid:

The UK spent £15.4 billion on overseas aid in 2023 — 1.2% of total government expenditure — not 10%.

Source: FCDO (2024).

“Scientific studies have shown that eating sugary foods causes children to become hyperactive and difficult to manage.”

Sugar and children’s behaviour:

Scientific research does not support the idea that sugar causes hyperactivity in children. A meta-analysis of 23 controlled trials found no evidence for this effect.

Source: Wolraich et al. (1995). JAMA 274(20):1617–1621.
